Underwriting Discipline in the AI Age

Chris Davis's Chairman's Desk Principles

"Technology shifts are constant, but the laws of microeconomics, return on invested capital (ROIC), and capital destruction are immutable."

Amara's Law

We tend to overestimate the effect of a technology in the short run and underestimate the effect in the long run. In the short run, valuations of "Enablers" expand speculatively; in the long run, "Users" capture structural savings.

Capital Cycle Theory

High returns on capital attract massive capital expenditures. As overinvestment occurs, commoditization begins, leading to severe down-cycles. Picking enablers at the peak of their pricing power is historically hazardous.

The Proprietary Data Moat

True users/winners are not pure software companies, but legacy firms with vast, closed-loop customer datasets (e.g., healthcare histories, transaction streams) that cannot be parsed by public search crawlers.

Critical S&P/NASDAQ Implementation Takeaways

Historically, major technological paradigm shifts—from railroads to the telegraph, and from the internet to smart mobile systems—rarely enrich the first-wave enablers long-term to the scale of early speculation. The ultimate winners are typically established firms that capture the productivity windfall.

The Passive Index Trap: Currently, the top-tier of the S&P 500 and the tech-heavy NASDAQ-100 are extremely concentrated in Category 1 ("The Enablers"). If the capital expenditures of hyper-scalers peak or experience downward revisions as they struggle to demonstrate immediate customer ROI, this index structure exposes passive investors to capital down-cycles.

Value Allocation Target: The sweet spot for active stock selection lies in identifying Category 2 ("The Users"). These are low-risk, cash-flowing companies trading at modest P/E multiples that can embed AI into their workflows to expand their operating margins without suffering massive competitive disruption.
